The premise

Why this series exists

What most people take to be established science, the expanding universe, the Big Bang, the inflation said to precede it, are narratives built in mathematics rather than findings pulled from measurement. Each piece of the standard picture was introduced to solve a problem created by the piece before it, and the result is a structure whose parts confirm each other without ever touching the ground.

The From Scratch series does not ask you to take that on faith. Each paper grants the standard framework its own assumptions, then tests whether those assumptions can actually be recovered from the data available. Where they cannot, the paper says so plainly and shows the work.

Mainstream journals evaluate foundational cosmology through referees working inside the very frameworks under examination. A paper questioning whether those frameworks were ever empirically warranted will not get a neutral hearing there. So the series is published independently, open to correspondence, and open to challenge.

“Before asking whether the universe is open, flat, or closed, there is a more pressing question: what observation tells us the universe has a global shape? None.”

The books

Three papers
Volume I

Nobody Observed an Expanding Universe

The FLRW Metric and the Origins of a Legend

The FLRW metric is treated as the physical shape of spacetime, yet its own architects, Friedmann, Lemaître, Robertson, and Walker, described it as a conditional mathematical construction, not a claim about the real universe. This paper goes back to their original papers, in their own words, and traces how a family of admissible mathematical worlds came to be mistaken for an established one.

Volume II

The Assumed Flatness of the Universe

Examining the Claim

Whether the universe is open, flat, or closed is treated as a settled empirical question. This paper grants that premise anyway and tests it directly, using a Gaussian-process reconstruction of the expansion rate from cosmic chronometers, without importing early-universe assumptions like the sound horizon. The result: late-time data alone cannot determine the universe's curvature at anything close to the precision usually claimed.

Volume III

The Sound Horizon

The Standard Ruler of Cosmology Has Never Been Empirically Identified

Nearly every modern distance measurement in cosmology is calibrated against the sound horizon, a ruler fixed by early-universe physics rather than measured directly. This paper examines whether that ruler has ever been independently identified in the data, or whether it has simply been assumed and then confirmed by the framework built to use it.

How to read the series

Each volume is a technical research paper. Conclusions are developed through mathematical analysis, observational reconstruction, statistical inference, and methodological examination, not through opinion or popular exposition.

You don't need to take the series' word for any of it. Every paper shows its data sources, its methods, and, where applicable, its code, so the analysis can be checked rather than trusted.

Start wherever your interest points: Volume I if you want the historical case, Volume II if you want the direct statistical test of flatness, Volume III if you want the standard ruler examined on its own.
